1
Foundation

Secure Organisational Commitment

Phase 1 will fail if it is treated as a side project. TYIF leadership must formally endorse the Idea & Project Space as a core organisational capability — not a pilot program, not a single team's initiative. This step must come first, before any tools are built or communications sent.

  • A
    Executive decision and mandate
    TYIF's CEO and senior leadership formally endorse the Idea & Project Space. This should be documented in writing — a board or leadership team resolution, a strategy paper, or a formal memo. Without this, the program has no authority to draw on organisational resources or ask facilitators to change their delivery.
  • B
    Appoint an Internal Program Lead
    Name one person responsible for Phase 1 operations. This role manages idea intake, coordinates facilitator training, maintains the review cycle, and reports progress to leadership. Without a named owner, accountability diffuses. This person does not need to be full-time on Phase 1, but it must be a formal part of their role description.
  • C
    Define resourcing and budget
    Identify minimum viable resourcing: time allocation for the Program Lead, basic tools (see Step 2), and facilitator training hours. Even a modest dedicated budget signals seriousness. Lack of any allocated resource communicates the opposite.
  • D
    Align brand leads across TYIF
    Schedule a short briefing with each TYIF brand and program lead. Explain what Phase 1 is, what it is not, what will be asked of their teams, and how it fits within their existing program structure. This builds alignment before anything is communicated to participants.
Key Principle
Phase 1 must be treated as infrastructure — like a CRM, an alumni database, or a staff training pathway. It requires consistent investment and stewardship, not a one-time launch.
2
Infrastructure

Build the Idea Capture System

The capture system is the backbone of Phase 1. It needs to be simple enough that a 16-year-old can use it during or after a program, and structured enough that ideas can be tracked, categorised, and responded to at scale. Start simple — a well-designed form is enough to begin.

Design the idea submission form
Keep it under 8 fields. Include: name (optional), program/brand, idea summary (2–3 sentences), problem being solved, who it affects, and whether they want to develop it further. Add one open field for anything else. Avoid jargon.
Choose and configure a platform
Options: Airtable (best for tracking + review), Notion, Google Forms + Sheets, or a simple web form linked to a shared inbox. Choose based on what TYIF staff already use — adoption is more important than features at this stage.
Build an idea categorisation system
Create 4–5 simple categories for incoming ideas: (1) Program improvement, (2) New community initiative, (3) Social enterprise concept, (4) For-profit concept, (5) Other / not sure. This enables routing and reporting without over-engineering the intake stage.
Set up acknowledgement and tracking
Every submission must receive a response — even a simple automated confirmation. Build a tracking spreadsheet or Airtable view with: submission date, category, status (received / under review / responded / actioned), and assigned reviewer. Unacknowledged ideas destroy trust.
Create a simple QR / short link
Generate a short, memorable URL (e.g. ideas.tyif.org.au or a Bitly link) and a QR code. These can be printed on program materials, handouts, and displayed at camps. Make submission frictionless.
Test with a small group first
Run a soft launch with one program cohort — 20–30 participants. Test the form, the acknowledgement flow, the review process, and the categorisation. Fix friction points before rolling out across all brands.
Avoid This
Do not over-engineer the system before you understand submission volume. A complex multi-stage tool used by nobody is worse than a basic form that 500 young people actually engage with.
3
People

Train Facilitators as the Front Line

Facilitators are the most important lever in Phase 1. They are the humans young people trust, and they are the ones who will — or won't — surface the Idea & Project Space during a program. Their understanding, tone, and confidence directly shapes participation rates. Facilitator training is non-negotiable.

  • A
    Create a facilitator briefing pack
    Produce a 1–2 page document for every facilitator that explains: what Phase 1 is, what it is not (no pressure, no equity, no obligation), how to introduce it in a session, what language to use, and how a participant submits an idea. Include the QR code and a sample script for introducing the concept.
  • B
    Run facilitator training sessions (30–45 min)
    Do this per-brand or per-cohort. Cover: the purpose and values behind Phase 1, how to have a natural conversation about ideas without creating unrealistic expectations, how to handle questions about what happens to an idea, and what not to say (especially around commercialisation or ownership).
  • C
    Build a standard program moment for idea capture
    Work with each brand's program designers to embed a dedicated moment in the program flow where ideas are invited. This could be a reflection exercise, a closing activity, or a challenge prompt. It should feel organic to the program, not like a separate form-filling exercise tagged on at the end.
  • D
    Equip facilitators to follow up individually
    When a facilitator identifies a participant with a particularly compelling or well-developed idea, they should be empowered to have a brief 1:1 conversation and personally encourage submission. Give facilitators a short follow-up script and a direct escalation contact (the Program Lead).

4
Program Ecosystem

Embed Across All TYIF Programs

Phase 1 must live inside existing programs — not alongside them. It should feel like a natural extension of what TYIF already does: inspiring, empowering, and encouraging young people to think about impact. The goal is seamless embedding, not extra workload.

  • A
    Map every current TYIF program and touchpoint
    Create a simple list of every program, camp, event, and cohort run across all TYIF brands. For each, note: program length, participant age range, existing reflection or creative activities, and the facilitator responsible. This becomes the integration roadmap.
  • B
    Design a per-program integration approach
    Each program is different. A 2-day camp needs a different approach than a 6-week youth leadership course. For shorter programs: embed a single 10-minute "What's Your Idea?" reflection near the close. For longer programs: introduce Phase 1 early, return to it mid-program, and close with structured idea submission.
  • C
    Add Phase 1 materials to all program collateral
    Every participant pack, workbook, closing handout, or digital resource should include a brief mention of the Idea & Project Space and the submission link/QR. Keep it simple: one paragraph, the link, and one line on what happens next.
  • D
    Create a dedicated "Idea Space" session template
    Build a reusable 15–20 minute facilitated session that any facilitator can run — with or without customisation. Include: an opening prompt ("What's a problem you keep thinking about?"), a pair-share activity, and a simple submission walkthrough. This becomes the default module used when time allows.
5
Alumni Ecosystem

Activate the Alumni Network

Alumni are a significant and underutilised asset. They have already been through TYIF programs, they have had time to reflect, and many will have developed or strengthened ideas since finishing. The alumni network extends Phase 1's reach far beyond active programs.

  • A
    Segment the alumni database by program and year
    Work with TYIF's existing alumni data to understand the size, recency, and program-of-origin of the alumni community. Segment into: recent alumni (within 2 years), older alumni, and highly engaged alumni. This allows targeted, relevant outreach rather than a single mass communication.
  • B
    Write and send an alumni launch communication
    Craft an email or message that explains what the Idea & Project Space is, why it exists, and how to submit an idea. Keep it personal in tone — this is not a newsletter, it is an invitation. Include a clear, honest explanation of what happens to ideas: they are reviewed, responded to, and some are developed further. No hype, no overpromising.
  • C
    Identify and brief Alumni Champions
    Select 5–10 engaged alumni across different programs and regions to act as informal champions. These are people already connected to TYIF who can organically spread awareness, answer questions from peers, and model what engagement looks like. Brief them individually, not in a group mass email.
  • D
    Embed Phase 1 in all future alumni touchpoints
    Any existing alumni events, newsletters, catch-ups, or digital communities (Facebook groups, LinkedIn pages, Discord) should include a standing mention of the Idea Space. Make it part of TYIF's alumni identity — not a one-off campaign.
6
Operations

Build the Review & Feedback Engine

The review and feedback process is what makes Phase 1 credible. If ideas go in and nothing comes back, trust collapses fast. Every submitted idea must receive a response. The quality of that response matters as much as the speed.

  • A
    Set a response SLA (service level agreement)
    Commit to a maximum response time for every idea — ideally within 5 business days. This does not need to be a detailed assessment; it can be a genuine acknowledgement and a short note on what happens next. What matters is that it happens consistently.
  • B
    Create a tiered response framework
    Tier 1 — acknowledge and encourage: most ideas receive this. A warm, personal response that validates the thinking and encourages continued development. Tier 2 — invite to develop: ideas showing early promise are invited to flesh out their concept further with a simple follow-up template. Tier 3 — escalate for Phase 2 consideration: rare ideas that clearly demonstrate readiness.
  • C
    Run a monthly idea review meeting
    The Program Lead and one or two staff members review all submissions monthly. This takes 30–45 minutes. The output: categorised ideas, updated statuses, any Tier 2 or Tier 3 candidates identified, and metrics updated. This keeps the pipeline moving without requiring real-time review of every idea.
  • D
    Close the loop on visible outcomes
    When an idea contributes to a program change, sparks a new initiative, or informs an organisational decision — communicate this. A brief mention in an alumni newsletter, a facilitator shout-out, or a direct message to the submitter: "Your idea led to X." This is the most powerful signal that Phase 1 is real.
Critical Insight
Young people do not need to see their idea become a venture to feel valued. Seeing a program activity tweaked, a new peer connection formed, or a question added to a camp program is enough to build lasting trust in the space.
7
Communication

Communicate with Clarity and Consistency

How Phase 1 is described matters enormously. The wrong framing — even a single word — can create false expectations about commercialisation, ownership, or outcomes. Every communication should be honest, warm, and expectation-appropriate.

Define approved language and phrases
Create a short style guide for anyone communicating about Phase 1. Include: what to say, what not to say, and example sentences. Share with all facilitators, brand managers, and comms staff before launch.
Create a simple FAQ for participants
Answer the 6–8 most common questions upfront: Who sees my idea? What happens to it? Do I own it? Will you use it? Will I get paid? Can I develop it myself? Post this at the submission link.
Produce a launch moment within TYIF
Don't just roll out quietly — create an internal launch moment with staff and facilitators first. A short briefing, an email from the CEO, or a team Slack post. Internal belief and buy-in is a prerequisite for external credibility.
Share early wins as case studies
When the first idea leads to a visible outcome — document it briefly. A two-paragraph story: what was the idea, who submitted it, what changed as a result. Use this in facilitator briefings and alumni comms to make Phase 1 feel real.

Measurement

Measure What Matters

Phase 1 does not need to produce ventures to be successful. Its success metrics are about engagement, quality of ideas, responsiveness, and the degree to which young people invest sustained effort — not commercial outcomes.

Report to leadership quarterly
Produce a simple 1-page quarterly report: total submissions, response rate, category breakdown, visible outcomes, and any Phase 2 candidates. This keeps leadership informed and maintains the organisational mandate for the program.
