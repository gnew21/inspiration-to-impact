1
Independence

Complete the Independence Checklist Before Phase 4 Begins

Phase 4 does not begin on a calendar date — it begins when a venture has genuinely reached operational independence. Every item on this checklist must be confirmed before TCII transitions from active support to advisory role.

Legal entity is fully operational
Company incorporated, ACN/ABN active, bank account operational, ASIC lodgements up to date, Shareholders Agreement signed by all parties, IP assigned correctly.
Board is functioning
Board meeting cadence established (quarterly minimum), minutes kept from Day 1, board roles clearly defined, and at least one director independent of the founding team.
First commercial revenue or partnership confirmed
At least one paying customer, signed partnership, or revenue-generating agreement in place. This is non-negotiable — the venture must have demonstrated real market demand before moving fully independent.
Financial controls established
Bookkeeping system active (Xero or equivalent), monthly P&L reviewed by the board, basic cash flow forecast maintained for 6 months forward, accountant engaged for tax obligations.
Operational systems in place
The venture can operate day-to-day without TCII's active involvement. Founders know their roles, key processes are documented, and the team is not dependent on TCII staff to function.
Value flow mechanism documented and agreed
The pre-agreed mechanism for value to flow back to TYIF via TCII is formally documented and understood by all parties. This is in writing — not a handshake or verbal understanding.
TCII's ongoing role formally defined
TCII's role in Phase 4 (advisory, board observer, or passive equity holder) is documented. Frequency of contact, reporting obligations, and circumstances for re-engagement are clearly defined.
TYIF relationship formally concluded
TYIF and the founders agree in writing that the venture is fully independent and no longer operating within TYIF's program or participant environment. This closes the formal TYIF relationship cleanly.
2
TCII Role Shift

Transition TCII from Active Support to Advisory Partner

TCII's role evolves significantly in Phase 4. The intensive hands-on support of Phase 3 is no longer appropriate — founders must own the decisions. TCII becomes a minority equity holder and available advisor, not an operator.

  • A
    Define TCII's board position clearly
    TCII holds a board observer seat (or a single non-executive director seat) — never a controlling position. TCII's representative attends all board meetings, contributes strategic perspective, and receives all board papers. They do not have veto rights, and they do not vote on ordinary resolutions. Their role is guidance, not governance.
  • B
    Establish a quarterly strategic touchpoint
    Once per quarter, a TCII representative meets with the founding team for a 60-minute strategic review. This is not a status update — it is a structured conversation about: growth trajectory, capital position, key risks, and whether TCII can add value in any specific area. TCII is proactive, not reactive.
  • C
    Make TCII's network actively available
    One of TCII's highest-value contributions in Phase 4 is warm introductions — to potential customers, strategic partners, investors, and talent. TCII should proactively identify relevant connections for each venture and make introductions without waiting to be asked. This is a core part of TCII's value.
  • D
    Define triggers for re-engagement
    Document the specific circumstances under which TCII would re-engage more actively: a significant pivot, a co-founder departure, a major legal or financial challenge, a fundraising round, or an acquisition conversation. Having these written removes ambiguity and ensures TCII can step in quickly when genuinely needed.
3
Value Flow

Activate the Value Flow Mechanisms to TYIF

This is the closing loop of the entire model. Value generated by successful ventures flows back to TYIF through TCII — strengthening the organisation that made those ideas possible. Every mechanism must be documented, transparent, and governed.

How value flows back to TYIF

Venture dividends
→
TCII receives its proportional share of any dividends declared by ventures in which it holds equity
Exit proceeds
→
On acquisition or IPO, TCII realises value from its equity stake. A pre-agreed proportion flows to TYIF as grants or endowment
TCII surplus
→
TCII's operating surplus (beyond what's needed to sustain and grow TCII's capacity) is allocated to TYIF as program grants
Strategic partnerships
→
Ventures may sponsor TYIF programs, fund camps, or partner on delivery as a strategic business decision — separate from equity flows
  • A
    Document the reinvestment mandate in the TYIF–TCII agreement
    The framework agreement must specify: the minimum proportion of TCII's realised proceeds that flows to TYIF, the mechanism (grant, endowment, direct funding), the frequency of transfer (annual, or upon realisation), and the governance process for allocation decisions. Vague language here will create conflict later.
  • B
    Establish TCII's annual reporting obligation to TYIF
    TCII provides TYIF with an annual summary of: portfolio venture performance, total value under management, any distributions made, and forecast value over the next 12 months. This is a governance document — TYIF's board should receive it annually. Transparency builds long-term trust between the organisations.
  • C
    Set realistic timelines for value realisation
    TYIF should not expect financial returns from Phase 4 ventures in the short term. Early-stage ventures typically take 5–10 years to generate significant returns. The model should be planned with this horizon in mind: TYIF invests in the model now, and benefits over the long term. Set this expectation with TYIF's board and funders explicitly.

Governance Principle

5
Growth Pathways

Support Ventures Across All Growth Pathways

Phase 4 ventures are not all the same. Some will pursue growth capital, others will run profitably without investors, some will be acquired, and some will close. All of these are legitimate outcomes. TCII's support must be calibrated to each venture's actual trajectory.

📈
Sustainable Profitability
The venture grows from revenue without external capital. Often the best outcome for impact-aligned businesses.
  • Focus on unit economics and gross margin
  • Reinvest operating surplus into growth
  • Build reserves before increasing team size
  • TCII role: quarterly strategy + introductions
🚀
Venture Capital Growth
For scalable models requiring significant upfront investment to capture market opportunity.
  • TCII prepares investor materials only after traction proven
  • Identify aligned investors — not just available capital
  • Negotiate terms carefully; protect founder control
  • Model TYIF value flow across dilution scenarios
🤝
Strategic Acquisition
A larger organisation acquires the venture — realising value for founders, TCII, and ultimately TYIF.
  • TCII advises on valuation and deal structure
  • Negotiate post-acquisition obligations carefully
  • Document value flow to TYIF at point of exit
  • Ensure mission alignment in acquirer selection
🔒
Orderly Closure
The venture closes responsibly. This is an expected and valid outcome — not a failure of the system.
  • Wind down cleanly with legal and financial advice
  • Settle all obligations before distributing assets
  • Document key learnings for TCII and TYIF
  • Celebrate what was built, regardless of outcome
6
Failure

Manage Failure Gracefully and Extract Its Value

Failure is not an edge case — it is a statistically likely outcome for early-stage ventures. The model must treat failure as a first-class outcome, not a shameful exception. How TYIF and TCII respond to failure will define whether young people trust the system over the long term.

  • A
    Define "failure" clearly — it is not the same as "closed"
    A venture that closes after running for 3 years, serving real customers, generating revenue, and developing its founders — is not a failure. A failure is a venture that closes without learning, leaves people worse off, or creates legal or reputational damage. TYIF and TCII should differentiate these clearly in all communications.
  • B
    Create a structured closure protocol
    When a venture chooses to close: (1) Notify TCII and TYIF in advance. (2) Engage a commercial lawyer to manage wind-down obligations. (3) Settle all creditor, employee, and tax obligations. (4) Document final learnings. (5) Communicate honestly with customers and stakeholders. (6) Hold a debrief with TCII within 60 days of closure.
  • C
    Extract and redistribute the learning
    Every closed venture produces a closure debrief that TCII files and shares (anonymised, with founder consent) with the broader ecosystem. What market assumption was wrong? What would the founders do differently? What did the venture teach about the problem it tried to solve? This learning strengthens future Phase 2 pilots and Phase 3 ventures.
  • D
    Support founders personally through closure
    Closing a company is one of the most difficult experiences a founder faces. TCII should proactively check in with founders after closure — not to assess the venture, but to support the people. A conversation, a connection to a new opportunity, or simply acknowledgement that the effort was real and valuable. This is how TCII earns long-term trust.
Reframe for the ecosystem
Founders who close a company responsibly and return to TYIF's broader network as experienced, commercially literate leaders are one of the most powerful outcomes of the entire system. They mentor Phase 1 participants, advise Phase 2 pilots, and model what leadership actually looks like.
7
Exit Planning

Plan for Exit Scenarios from Day One

Exit planning is not morbid — it is responsible. A venture that has never discussed exit scenarios is not prepared for the decisions that will arise when an acquisition offer comes, when a co-founder wants to leave, or when investors push for a liquidity event. TCII should initiate exit scenario planning within 12–18 months of Phase 4 launch.

Map the founder exit provisions in the SHA
The Shareholders Agreement should already contain: drag-along and tag-along rights, right of first refusal on share transfers, leaver provisions (good and bad leaver clauses), and a pre-agreed valuation mechanism or process for resolving disputes.
Model value flows at exit for all parties
Run a simple financial model at 3–4 hypothetical exit valuations (e.g. $2M, $5M, $15M, $50M). Show each party's return: founders, TCII, and the resulting flow to TYIF. This makes the model tangible and builds shared understanding before any pressure arises.
Define strategic acquirer categories
Identify the types of organisations that might be natural acquirers of this venture: strategic corporates, not-for-profits, private equity, or other founders. Having a rough list prepared means founders are not starting from zero when an approach is made.
Align founders on exit philosophy early
Co-founders often have very different views on what a "good" exit looks like. Surface these differences early, in a structured conversation facilitated by TCII — while the relationships are strong and there is no live deal on the table. Misalignment discovered during a deal is far more damaging.
8
The Flywheel

Build the Flywheel That Makes the System Self-Reinforcing

Phase 4 is not the end of the model — it is the stage where the model becomes self-reinforcing. Successful ventures, returned value, experienced founders, and visible proof all feed back into Phases 1 and 2, making the entire system stronger and more credible over time.

  • A
    Route returned value directly into TYIF program investment
    When TYIF receives its first financial return from the system — however small — make it visible. Announce that new program funding, a new camp, or a new staff role was made possible by the reinvestment from a venture that started as a young person's idea. This is the most powerful story the model can tell.
  • B
    Bring Phase 4 founders back as mentors and advisors
    Founders who have been through the full system — whether they succeeded or closed — are the most credible possible advocates for Phase 1 and Phase 2. Invite them to speak at TYIF programs, facilitate idea sessions, advise Innovation Lab pilots, and sit on TCII's advisory panel. Their presence makes the model real for every new participant.
  • C
    Publish an annual system impact report
    Each year, TYIF and TCII jointly produce a brief public-facing impact report: total ideas submitted in Phase 1, Phase 2 pilots run, Phase 3 ventures launched, Phase 4 ventures operating, financial value returned to TYIF, and stories from the founders who built something. This report builds public trust, supports fundraising, and attracts the next generation of participants.
  • D
    Use system performance data to improve each phase
    The annual system-wide review is the mechanism for continuous improvement. What types of Phase 1 ideas tend to progress? What pilot designs work best in Phase 2? What venture structures are most successful in Phase 3? These patterns should actively inform how each phase is designed and refined year on year.
