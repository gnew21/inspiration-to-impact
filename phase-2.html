1
Governance

Establish the Governance Structure

The Innovation Lab requires a small, clearly defined review group with real authority. Without clear governance, the lab becomes a holding space for unfinished ideas and informal influence. Governance must be established before any projects enter the lab.

  • A
    Form the Innovation Lab Review Group
    This group has 3–5 members: TYIF CEO or delegate (Chair), a TYIF program lead, a TYIF operations/finance representative, and optionally one external advisor with relevant experience. Keep it small — large committees dilute decision-making. TCII may observe but does not vote at this stage.
  • B
    Define the Review Group's authority
    The Review Group has authority to: approve or reject Phase 2 entry, set pilot parameters (scope, timeline, resources), make end-of-pilot decisions (integrate, close, or advance), and allocate TYIF's Phase 2 budget. Document this formally — authority without written definition gets challenged.
  • C
    Set a regular cadence
    The Review Group meets monthly, or whenever a pilot reaches a decision point. Each meeting follows a standard agenda: active pilot updates, end-of-phase decisions pending, new Phase 1 candidates to assess. Keep meetings to 60–90 minutes maximum.
  • D
    Appoint an Innovation Lab Coordinator
    A named staff member (separate from the Review Group) coordinates day-to-day lab operations: project scheduling, communication with participants, documentation, and meeting preparation. The Review Group makes decisions; the Coordinator keeps everything moving between meetings.

2
Selection

Design the Entry Criteria and Assessment Process

Entry into Phase 2 is earned — not given. Clear, written criteria make selection fair, defensible, and trustworthy. Vague criteria lead to inconsistent decisions and erode confidence in the system. The assessment should take minutes to apply and hours to dispute — not the reverse.

  • A
    Define the four core entry criteria (all must be met)
    1. Mission alignment — Does the idea directly or indirectly serve TYIF's purpose of strengthening youth impact?

    2. Problem clarity — Can the team articulate the specific problem, who experiences it, and why existing solutions are insufficient?

    3. Feasibility — Can this be meaningfully tested within TYIF's operating environment, available resources, and a defined timeframe?

    4. Ethical safety — Does the idea raise any concerns about safeguarding, legal compliance, data privacy, or reputational risk?
  • B
    Build a simple scoring template
    Create a one-page assessment form for Review Group members. For each criterion: a 1–3 rating and a short written rationale. This forces reviewers to articulate their reasoning and prevents gut-feel decisions. Aggregate scores should inform, not replace, a genuine conversation.
  • C
    Design a Phase 1 → Phase 2 nomination pathway
    Ideas can be nominated for Phase 2 by: the Phase 1 Program Lead (based on review data), a facilitator who worked with the team, or the participant team themselves (self-nomination). All nominations go through the same assessment process. No informal fast-tracking — even strong ideas go through the gate.
  • D
    Create a brief candidate interview process
    Before final approval, the Review Group or Coordinator holds a short conversation (30–45 min) with the project team. This is not a high-pressure pitch — it is a genuine assessment of the team's understanding of their idea, their commitment to a defined pilot, and their comfort with accountability. If the team cannot articulate what they are testing, they are not ready.
Selection Risk
The biggest failure mode is selecting too many projects at once. Start with 1–2 pilots maximum in the first cohort. Quality, focus, and visible outcomes matter far more than volume. An overfull lab with underdeveloped projects damages credibility.
3
Environment

Build the Lab Environment

The Innovation Lab doesn't need a physical space — it needs a structured operating environment: tools, rituals, templates, and norms that make pilot work feel real, distinct, and accountable. This is what separates the lab from a classroom project.

Set up a shared project workspace
Each project gets a dedicated shared folder or Notion/Google Drive workspace: project brief, pilot plan, meeting notes, evidence log, and a weekly update log. The Coordinator has view access to all workspaces at all times.
Establish a weekly check-in rhythm
Each project team has a fortnightly 30-minute check-in with the Coordinator. Agenda: what did you do, what did you learn, what's next, any blockers? This keeps momentum without over-managing. Red flags (inactivity, scope creep, team conflict) surface here first.
Create a Pilot Brief template
Every project entering Phase 2 completes a Pilot Brief: problem statement, proposed solution, what will be tested, how success will be measured, timeline, resources needed, and defined exit decision date. One page maximum. This becomes the project's contract with the Review Group.
Define available resources per project
Be explicit: what TYIF can provide (staff time, access to programs, meeting space, small budget if applicable) and what it cannot (equity, IP ownership, guarantee of outcomes). Projects must work within defined constraints — this tests real-world feasibility.
Build a mentorship/advisory connection
Each project team should have access to one relevant advisor (internal TYIF staff or a vetted external advisor) who can provide expertise relevant to their domain. This is not coaching — it is a specific knowledge resource the team can consult as needed.
Establish a "lab norm" document
A short written agreement all participants sign on entering Phase 2: what the lab is, how decisions are made, how learning is shared, confidentiality expectations, and what happens when a pilot closes. Clarity upfront prevents confusion and hurt later.
4
Delivery

Run the 8–12 Week Pilot Framework

Every project in Phase 2 follows the same structured pilot arc. The timeline is intentionally short — long enough to generate real evidence, short enough to maintain discipline. Projects that need significantly longer are usually not ready for Phase 2.

Weeks
1–2
Define & Align
  • Finalise and sign the Pilot Brief
  • Agree success metrics with the Review Group
  • Set the explicit exit date and decision criteria
  • Identify resources, advisors, and access needed
  • Brief all stakeholders involved in the pilot
Weeks
3–6
Build & Test
  • Execute the defined test: build the prototype, run the session, survey the cohort, test the service, etc.
  • Document evidence as you go — not retrospectively
  • Fortnightly Coordinator check-ins and written updates
  • Identify and escalate blockers early
  • Mid-point Review Group touchpoint (week 5 or 6)
Weeks
7–10
Gather & Synthesise
  • Complete the primary test phase
  • Gather qualitative and quantitative data against success metrics
  • Hold structured reflection sessions with the project team
  • Prepare a short end-of-pilot Evidence Report (see Step 7)
  • Identify any new questions raised by the evidence
Weeks
11–12
Review & Decide
  • Present Evidence Report to the Review Group
  • Review Group makes a formal exit decision: integrate, close, or advance to Phase 3
  • Communicate decision clearly and promptly to the project team
  • Begin transition activities based on outcome
  • File all learning into TYIF's knowledge base
Key Discipline
The exit date is fixed. It does not move because a project is "nearly there" or because a team is enthusiastic. If a project is not ready to produce evidence within 12 weeks, the correct decision is to close it, document what was learned, and allow a fresh entry if the team returns with a better-defined question.
5
Evidence

Design Success Metrics for Each Pilot

Success metrics must be defined at the start of the pilot — not assessed at the end. Projects that define their own success criteria after seeing the results are not generating evidence; they are constructing justifications. The Review Group approves metrics at kick-off, before any work begins.

  • A
    Require each project to propose their own metrics first
    Before the Review Group sets metrics, the project team proposes what success looks like for them. This reveals how well they understand their own idea. Vague proposals ("we want to see if people like it") are a signal the team needs more Phase 1 development time.
  • B
    Require two types of metrics: impact and feasibility
    Impact metrics: evidence that the solution addresses the problem (e.g., participant feedback scores, behaviour change indicators, engagement data).

    Feasibility metrics: evidence that the solution can actually work within TYIF's environment (e.g., delivery cost, staff time required, scalability indicators).
  • C
    Add one "killer question" per project
    Each project should identify the single question that, if answered negatively, should cause the pilot to stop. This is the most important metric. If the answer to the killer question is "no", it does not matter how encouraging the other data looks. This forces intellectual honesty.
6
Decision

Run a Clean Exit Decision Process

The end-of-pilot decision is the most important moment in Phase 2. It must be made clearly, promptly, and based on evidence — not on enthusiasm, relationship, or sunk cost. Stopping a project should feel like a success, not a failure.

Outcome 1: Integrate into TYIF
The pilot has demonstrated clear value and the solution fits naturally within TYIF's existing programs or operations. The Review Group approves integration, assigns ownership to a program team, and removes the project from the lab. Clear, bounded success.
Outcome 2: Close with learning retained
The pilot did not demonstrate sufficient feasibility or impact to warrant further development within TYIF. The project closes. The Evidence Report is filed. The team receives a genuine, respectful debrief. All learning is retained and may inform future pilots.
Outcome 3: Eligible for Phase 3 assessment
The pilot has produced clear evidence of real-world value that cannot or should not continue within TYIF. The Review Group formally nominates it for Phase 3 assessment by TCII. This is rare — reserved for the very few ideas that clearly demonstrate independent commercial potential.
Document and communicate every decision formally
Every exit decision must be documented in writing and communicated to the project team with a clear rationale. No ambiguous "we'll revisit this" — each pilot gets a clear, written outcome. This is what makes the system trustworthy over time.
Governance Risk
The most common failure is allowing projects to remain in Phase 2 indefinitely — becoming "zombie pilots" that consume attention without producing decisions. If a project has not met its exit date criteria, close it. The team can re-enter if they develop a more focused question.
7
Knowledge

Retain and Use What You Learn

The Innovation Lab generates significant organisational intelligence — about youth needs, program gaps, emerging ideas, and what works in real-world contexts. This learning has value independent of whether any project becomes a venture. It must be deliberately captured and used.

  • A
    Require an Evidence Report from every pilot
    2–4 pages. Covers: the question tested, how it was tested, what the evidence showed, what the team learned, and what they would do differently. This is produced by the project team and reviewed by the Coordinator. Filed into a shared TYIF knowledge library regardless of outcome.
  • B
    Run a "Labs Learning" debrief each half-year
    Every 6 months, the Review Group and Coordinator hold a 90-minute retrospective on all completed pilots. What patterns emerged? What gaps did pilots expose in current programs? What types of ideas are consistently strong or weak? This feeds directly into TYIF's program development and Phase 1 improvement.
  • C
    Share anonymised learnings with facilitators and staff
    Closed pilots should not disappear silently. Share brief summaries (with participant consent) with TYIF's broader facilitator community: "We tested X, here's what we learned." This demonstrates that the system takes ideas seriously even when they don't progress — which strengthens Phase 1 participation.
8
Advancement

Trigger the Phase 3 Pathway Correctly

The transition to Phase 3 is the rarest outcome of the Innovation Lab. It must be treated as an exceptional event — not a default aspiration. Getting this transition wrong has consequences for TYIF's charitable integrity. Getting it right creates a durable, ethical pipeline to independent venture formation.

  • A
    Confirm all Phase 2 conditions are formally met
    Before initiating Phase 3 contact, the Review Group formally confirms in writing: Phase 2 is complete, there is documented evidence the project cannot or should not continue within TYIF, and all participants are willing and appropriate to transition from program participants to independent founders.
  • B
    Initiate a formal TCII referral
    The Review Group formally refers the project to TCII with the completed Evidence Report and Pilot Brief. TCII then runs its own independent assessment — TYIF's recommendation is not a guarantee of Phase 3 entry. Both organisations assess independently.
  • C
    Ensure all participants receive independent guidance
    Before any Phase 3 transition, TYIF must encourage all participants to seek independent legal advice on what it means to become a founder, take equity, and separate from the charity. TYIF should facilitate access to this guidance without pressure or direction about outcomes.
